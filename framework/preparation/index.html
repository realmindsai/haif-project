Phase 2: Preparation

Develop strategies to leverage enablers and address barriers identified during Exploration.

Bottom Line In this phase, the team turns Phase 1 findings into actionable plans: selecting the right intervention, determining timing, identifying suitable patient groups, revising hospital guidelines, and assigning clear roles. Thorough preparation is what separates a successful rollout from a stalled pilot.

Overview

Phase 2 bridges the gap between understanding (Exploration) and action (Implementation). The team develops strategies to use enablers and address barriers identified in Phase 1. While this page uses PONV as the primary worked example, every step applies to any hospital acupuncture or acupressure programme.

Key Preparation Items

  • Identify appropriate timing for acupuncture/acupressure intervention
  • Revise or update hospital guidelines to incorporate the intervention
  • Identify and revise relevant clinical documents
  • Determine each team member's role (using the CFIR framework)
  • Define suitable patient types and procedures
  • Address identified barriers with targeted strategies
  • Determine who will deliver the intervention, based on timing and setting

1. Feasibility of the Selected Intervention

FAME Criteria Assessment

The FAME framework (Pearson et al., 2005) provides a structured way to evaluate whether an intervention should proceed to implementation. Each criterion should be assessed for your specific clinical context:

CriterionQuestionPONV Example Assessment
Feasibility Can it be done in this setting with available resources? Acupressure wristbands require minimal training and ~$5 per unit
Appropriateness Does it fit the clinical context and patient population? Non-invasive, patient-acceptable, aligns with multimodal guidelines
Meaningfulness Is it valued by patients and clinicians? 87% patient willingness post-education; 81% of staff would encourage patients
Effectiveness Does the evidence support clinical benefit? Cochrane-level evidence: RR 0.68 nausea, 0.60 vomiting High

For PONV, the assessment concludes that acupressure is a feasible, appropriate, meaningful, and effective intervention that should be implemented. Apply this same framework to your target condition.

2. Timing Considerations

When to Deliver the Intervention

The timing of acupuncture/acupressure delivery depends on the clinical purpose:

PurposeTimingConsiderations
Prevention Pre-operative or immediate post-operative Often provided 30 min prior to surgery, or potentially 12–24 hours beforehand
Management After symptoms appear Can be used as adjunct to rescue medication or as standalone intervention

Timing decisions are influenced by staff availability, surgical scheduling, ward workflows, and which staff members will deliver the intervention. For non-surgical applications (e.g., emergency department), timing aligns with patient presentation and triage flow.

3. Patient Risk Factors and Selection

Identifying Suitable Patient Groups (PONV Example)

For PONV specifically, high-risk indicators include:

  • Female gender
  • Prior PONV or motion sickness history
  • Non-smoker status
  • Younger age

Surgery types with elevated risk include:

  • Cholecystectomy
  • Gynaecological procedures
  • Laparoscopic procedures

For other clinical applications, define equivalent inclusion criteria based on condition prevalence, severity, and the populations most likely to benefit from acupuncture/acupressure.

4. Documentation and Guidelines

Documents Requiring Revision

Integrating acupuncture or acupressure into hospital practice typically requires updates to:

  • Hospital clinical guidelines — to include acupuncture/acupressure as a treatment option
  • Clinical checklists — e.g., PONV risk checklists, pre-operative assessment forms
  • Patient education materials — information sheets, consent forms
  • Operative documentation — to record intervention delivery
  • Ward documentation — nursing observation charts
  • Drug charts — if the intervention may reduce pharmacological requirements

Engage your hospital's document governance process early — approval timelines can add weeks or months to your implementation schedule.

5. Addressing Barriers

Primary Barriers and Strategies

Phase 1 data (staff surveys, readiness assessment) typically surfaces four key barriers. The table below maps each barrier to preparation strategies:

BarrierPreparation Strategy
Lack of awareness regarding existing evidence Prepare evidence summaries; schedule educational sessions; identify opinion leaders to champion the evidence base
Insufficient capability Design training packages tailored to each professional group; arrange competency assessments; plan on-the-job mentoring
Equipment unavailability Source equipment and consumables; negotiate procurement; establish supply chain for ongoing use
Time constraints Map intervention delivery onto existing workflows; identify time-neutral integration points; present time-burden data (only 38% of surveyed staff viewed acupuncture as too time-consuming)

6. Team Role Assignment

Defining Roles Using the CFIR Framework

The Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR) provides a structured approach to defining team roles. For each phase of care, clarify:

  • Who delivers the intervention (e.g., nurse, acupuncturist, anaesthetist)
  • Who monitors the patient during and after delivery
  • Who documents the intervention and outcomes
  • Who escalates if adverse events occur
  • Who audits compliance and outcomes

Role assignment should align with the timing decisions made above. Pre-operative delivery may involve different staff than ward-based management.
