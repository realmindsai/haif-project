Phase 3: Implementation

Execute the plan using evidence-based implementation strategies, training, management engagement, and staged rollout.

Bottom Line Successful hospital acupuncture implementation requires coordinating both internal factors (organisational structure, communication, readiness) and external factors (patient needs, peer networks, policy). Use proven implementation strategies — opinion leaders are the most effective at 12% behaviour change — and start with a controlled pilot before scaling.

Overview

Successful acupuncture/acupressure implementation requires both internal and external enablers. Internal factors encompass organisation characteristics, networks and communication, climate for change, and readiness for change. External factors cover patient needs, peer pressure, organisational networks, and external policy.

This phase translates the preparation work into action, using the Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR) constructs as a guide. While PONV acupressure serves as the worked example, the strategies apply to any hospital acupuncture programme.

1. Structure of the Organisation

CFIR: Structural Characteristics

Implementation scope determines which departments and units must be involved. The broader the intervention window, the more coordination is required.

PONV example: If acupressure wristbands are used for 24 hours peri-surgically, then staff from admission, theatre, recovery, and the wards will all be involved. Coordination and communication among those units are essential.

General principle: Ensure all key stakeholders are part of the team. Map the patient journey through your organisation and identify every handover point where the intervention must be communicated, maintained, or documented.

2. Priority and Goals

Aligning Implementation with Clinician Priorities

Research shows that alignment between an intervention and existing clinician priorities is a stronger predictor of implementation effectiveness than the strength of the evidence itself.

To achieve alignment:

  • Communicate how the intervention supports goals staff already care about (e.g., patient satisfaction, reduced medication burden, shorter recovery times)
  • Provide regular feedback on outcomes that matter to frontline clinicians
  • Frame the intervention as enhancing — not replacing — existing practice

3. Implementation Strategies

Evidence-Based Strategies (Grimshaw, 2012)

A systematic review by Grimshaw (2012) quantified the effectiveness of common implementation strategies. The table below shows each strategy with its measured effect and a hospital acupuncture example:

Strategy Effectiveness Hospital Acupuncture Example
Printed educational material 4.3% Evidence summary handouts, point location guides, patient information sheets distributed to all relevant clinical areas
Educational meetings 6% Grand rounds presentations, ward in-service sessions, lunchtime seminars covering evidence, technique, and workflow integration
Local opinion leaders 12% High Respected senior clinicians who advocate for acupuncture/acupressure, demonstrate technique, and mentor colleagues
Audit and feedback 5% Regular reports on intervention uptake, patient outcomes, and compliance fed back to clinical teams

Effectiveness refers to median absolute improvement in compliance with desired practice. Strategies are most effective when combined.

4. Training

CFIR: Access to Knowledge and Information

Training is essential for all staff involved in delivery, monitoring, and documentation. The POPA4Ease project developed three differentiated training packages:

PackageAudienceContent Focus
Package A Direct intervention deliverers (nurses, acupuncturists) Point location, technique, dosage, contraindications, documentation
Package B Supervising clinicians (anaesthetists, surgeons) Evidence base, patient selection, safety monitoring, escalation protocols
Package C Ward and support staff Awareness of intervention, monitoring for displacement or adverse events, documentation requirements

Supplement formal training with on-the-job mentoring: pair trained staff with new adopters for the first several patients.

5. Engaging Management

CFIR: Leadership Engagement

Leadership engagement — defined as the commitment, involvement, and accountability of leaders and managers — is required at three levels:

LevelRole in Implementation
Executive leadership Authorise the programme, allocate resources, signal organisational priority
Middle management Facilitate cross-departmental coordination, resolve operational barriers
Frontline management Support daily practice, adjust rosters, reinforce training, provide immediate feedback

6. Assessment and Evaluation

CFIR: Reflecting and Evaluating

Define outcome measures before launch. Decide what to measure, how to track it, and how often to review:

  • Clinical outcomes — condition incidence rates, rescue medication use, patient-reported outcomes
  • Process measures — intervention uptake rate, correct technique adherence, documentation completeness
  • Staff measures — confidence levels, satisfaction, feedback on workflow integration
  • Patient measures — satisfaction, willingness to recommend, adverse events

Use regular audits to identify and correct implementation drift early. The Joanna Briggs Institute's PACES online audit tool provides a structured approach to clinical audit.

7. Execute the Implementation

Staged Rollout Approach

Rather than organisation-wide launch, use a staged approach to manage risk and build confidence:

Step 1: Practice Runs

Conduct dry runs with simulated patients or willing volunteers. Identify workflow issues, timing problems, and documentation gaps before any real patient contact.

Step 2: Pilot Implementation

Start with a single ward, unit, or surgical list. Collect data intensively, debrief weekly, and refine protocols based on real-world experience.

Step 3: Controlled Expansion

Expand to additional units one at a time, using lessons from the pilot. Each new unit benefits from refined protocols and trained mentors from earlier stages.

Tip: Limiting initial scope reduces resistance and allows early adopters to become advocates before sceptical colleagues must engage.
