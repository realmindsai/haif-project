## HOME PAGE

**Site Title:** POPA4Ease - Post-Operative Acupuncture for Easing Post Op Complications

**Purpose Statement:**
"Post-operative nausea and vomiting (PONV) is common. Modern antiemetic medications have improved its prevention and management. Nevertheless one in three surgical patients still suffer from PONV."

The site assists clinicians including anaesthetists, surgeons, nurses, and acupuncturists in developing implementation plans across four stages: Exploration, Preparation, Implementation, and Sustainment.

**Definition of Acupuncture and Acupressure:**
According to WHO, acupuncture involves "stimulating body points in an organised manner for therapeutic purposes" through needling, electrical stimulation, pressure, heat, or chemical methods. The site defines acupuncture as needle-based invasive stimulation, while acupressure refers to "non-invasive mechanical pressure applied onto body points."

**Key Facts About PONV:**
- Affects 30-50% of surgical patients
- Rated by patients as more important to avoid than post-operative pain
- Impacts recovery and prolongs hospital stays
- Acupuncture/acupressure are "the only non-pharmacological interventions included in the PONV management guidelines"

**Framework Rationale:**
The site notes that evidence for acupuncture has not been "translated into clinical practice in a systematic manner." The four-phase framework (EPIS model) addresses barriers and enablers for implementation.

## PHASE 1: EXPLORATION

**Primary Objective:** "Identifying human factors and environmental factors"

Before implementation, organizations must understand both human and environmental contexts, including:
- Internal need for change existence
- Available intervention types
- Implementation team composition
- Staff perspectives on proposed changes
- Patient requirements
- Funding necessity and availability
- Organizational readiness for change

### 1. Identify Internal Needs

Using PONV (postoperative nausea and vomiting) as example:

**How is PONV prevented and managed locally?**
- Review hospital practice guidelines for PONV management

**Is PONV incidence charted?**
- Check observation charts in Recovery, ward, and pre-discharge phases
- Verify documentation across all care settings

**What is current PONV incidence and rescue antiemetic usage?**
- Track current incident rates
- Monitor rescue medication administration
- Note: "rescue anti-emetic use is not equivalent to PONV"
- Resource: PONV study data extraction template provided

**Is PONV risk assessed?**
- "PONV risk assessment is the first recommendation for PONV management" (Gan 2014)
- Four primary risk factors identified:
  - Female gender
  - Non-smoker status
  - Previous PONV or motion sickness history
  - Post-operative opioid use
- Risk increases 20% per additional factor (Apfel et al., 1999)
- Patient self-assessment checklist available

### 2. Intervention Types: Acupuncture/Acupressure Modalities

**Manual Acupressure**
- Advantages: Avoids needles; requires no equipment; easily trained staff
- Disadvantages: Effectiveness varies by practitioner skill

**Electroacupressure (E-Stim)**
- Effectiveness: Nausea reduced 45% to 30% (RR 0.71, 95% CI 0.62-0.81); Vomiting reduced 30% to 18% (RR 0.60, 95% CI 0.50-0.73)
- Advantages: Extended-duration capability; adjustable intensity
- Disadvantages: Equipment cost ($40-$200); training required

**Auricular Acupuncture (Ear)**
- Advantages: Economical (few cents per needle); individualized point selection; low needle misplacement risk
- Disadvantages: Staff training required; qualified acupuncturist may be needed
- Effectiveness: Effective for gynecological surgery and laparoscopic cholecystectomy

**Body Acupuncture (PC6 + Other Points)**
- Effectiveness: Nausea reduced 54% to 29% (RR 0.56, 95% CI 0.39-0.80); Vomiting reduced 41% to 20% (RR 0.51, 95% CI 0.34-0.76)
- Combined therapy: PC6 + acupoints reduced PONV 21% to 6% (RR 0.29, 95% CI 0.17-0.49)
- Advantages: Economical ($0.05 per needle); individualized selection
- Disadvantages: Training requirement; needle monitoring needed

**Electroacupuncture (EA)**
- Effectiveness: Equivalent to body acupuncture with adjustment capability
- Advantages: Reusable equipment; adjustable stimulus intensity
- Disadvantages: Higher equipment cost ($200-$800); monitoring required

**Acupressure Wristbands**
- Effectiveness: Nausea reduced 36% to 24% (RR 0.60, 95% CI 0.53-0.69); Vomiting reduced 20% to 15% (RR 0.54, 95% CI 0.45-0.64); Rescue antiemetics reduced 22% to 13% (RR 0.62, 95% CI 0.52-0.74)
- Advantages: Cost-effective (~$5 AUD); extended-use capability; no infection risk; minimal training; flexible delivery; home-use continuation
- Disadvantages: Limited sizing for large wrists; impractical with multiple IV lines; potential wrist injuries

**PC6 Acupoint Stimulation (All Modalities Combined)**
- Effectiveness: Nausea 47% to 31% (RR 0.68, 95% CI 0.60-0.77); Vomiting 33% to 19% (RR 0.60, 95% CI 0.51-0.71); Rescue antiemetics 33% to 20% (RR 0.64, 95% CI 0.55-0.73)
- Advantages: Mild, transient side effects; equivalent to common antiemetics; enhanced efficacy when combined with drugs
- Disadvantages: Staff training required

### 3. The Implementation Team

Team composition should include:

**Opinion Leaders/Facilitators:** "Individuals in an organization who have formal or informal influence on attitudes and beliefs regarding intervention implementation"

**Champions:** Individuals who "drive through an implementation, overcoming indifference or resistance"

**External Change Agents:** External individuals who "formally influence or facilitate intervention decisions"

Key principle: Early users should share professional, educational, cultural, or socioeconomic backgrounds with leadership for successful implementation.

### 4. Staff Assessment

Staff groups requiring involvement:
- Anaesthetists
- Nurses
- Midwives
- Gynaecologists/Obstetricians
- Surgeons
- Administrative clerks
- Interpreters

**USA Survey Results (Faircloth, 2014):**
- Respondents: 292 from 1,728 anaesthesia staff across 96 departments
- Composition: 54% anaesthesiologists, 44% nurses, 2% aides
- Prior use with positive outcomes: 27%
- Willing to consider use: 54%
- Willing to pursue education: 74%
- Main barriers: "Lack of scientific evidence" (79%), "Unavailability of credentialed providers" (71%), "Lack of equipment" (49%)
- Time concern: Only 38% viewed acupuncture as too time-consuming

**Australian Hospital Survey Results:**
- Respondents: 165 doctors, nurses, midwives, obstetricians
- Believed effective for PONV: 42%
- Would encourage patients after education: 81%
- Wanted further education: 88%

**Australian General Practice Context:**
- National survey of 544 GPs: ~85% considered acupuncture effective and safe for primary care (Cohen et al., 2005)
- GP referral rate: 80% have referred patients (Wardle et al., 2013)
- Medical school integration: Some Australian schools teach acupuncture as electives

### 5. Organizational Readiness Assessment

**Tool:** Modified Checklist to Assess Organization Readiness (CARI) (Barwick, 2011)

Evaluates:
- System readiness
- Senior leadership support
- Staff capability
- Implementation plan maturity
- Training requirements

Scoring: Total possible = 100 points
Readiness threshold: 80+ points (minimum 15 points per category)

### 6. Cost and Funding Considerations

**Cost Assessment Framework (Myle, 2016):**

Costs to Track:
- Device cost per patient
- Staff time for intervention delivery
- Staff time for intervention monitoring
- Prevention antiemetic medications and costs
- Rescue antiemetic medications and costs
- Intra-operative and post-operative pain medications and costs

Assessment Methods:
- Track intervention delivery time (minutes)
- Document medication type and dosage
- Calculate pharmaceutical costs
- Calculate device costs
- Compare cost-effectiveness against alternatives

**Cost Comparison:**
- Acupressure wristbands: ~$5 AUD per unit (reusable)
- Acupuncture needles: ~$0.05 per needle
- Electroacupressure units: $40-$200
- Electroacupuncture units: $200-$800

### 7. Patient Perspectives

**Patient Survey Results (Weeks et al., 2017):**
- Sample: 160 surgical patients (Australian)
- Pre-education willingness: 65% would use acupuncture for PONV
- Baseline knowledge: Only 15% knew acupuncture could treat nausea/vomiting
- Post-education willingness: 87% (22-percentage-point increase)

PONV Management Guideline Recommendation: Interventions should consider patient preferences when selecting treatment approaches.

## PHASE 2: PREPARATION

**Overview:** "In this Phase, the team will develop strategies to use enablers and address barriers identified in Phase 1."

### Key Phase 2 Items
- Identify appropriate timing for acupuncture/acupressure intervention
- Revise or update hospital guidelines
- Identify and revise relevant documents
- Determine each team member's role (using CFIR framework)
- Define suitable patient types and surgical procedures
- Address identified barriers
- Determine intervention delivery personnel based on timing

### Feasibility of the Selected Intervention
References the FAME criteria (Feasibility, Appropriateness, Meaningfulness, Effectiveness) developed by Pearson et al. (2005). Assessment concludes that "acupressure is a feasible, appropriate, meaningful and effective intervention that should be implemented for PONV."

### Timing Considerations
Intervention timing depends on purpose: prevention requires pre-operative or immediate post-operative application, while management occurs after symptoms appear. Acupuncture is "often provided 30 min prior to surgery" or potentially 12-24 hours beforehand, with timing influenced by staff availability and surgical scheduling.

### Patient Risk Factors
High-risk PONV indicators include female gender, prior PONV/motion sickness history, non-smoker status, and younger age. Surgery types with elevated risk include cholecystectomy, gynecological, and laparoscopic procedures.

### Implementation Requirements
- Hospital PONV guidelines revision
- PONV checklists
- Patient education materials
- Operative and ward documentation updates
- Drug chart modifications

### Primary Barriers Identified
Four key obstacles:
1. Lack of awareness regarding existing evidence
2. Insufficient capability
3. Equipment unavailability
4. Time constraints

## PHASE 3: IMPLEMENTATION

**Introduction:** Successful acupuncture/acupressure implementation requires both internal and external enablers. Internal factors encompass five domains: "organisation characteristics, network and communication, climate for change and readiness for change," while external factors cover patient needs, peer pressure, organizational networks, and external policy.

### Structure of the Organisation
References CFIR's "Structural Characteristics," discussing how implementation scope determines departmental involvement. "If acupressure is used for 24 hours peri-surgically, then staff from admission, theatre, recovery to the wards will be involved." "Coordination and communication among those units are essential" and recommends ensuring "all key stakeholders are part of the team."

### Priority and Goals
Addresses clinician priorities alignment with implementation, described as "a stronger predictor of the effectiveness of implementation." Advocates communicating goals and providing feedback aligned with staff objectives.

### Implementation Strategies
Grimshaw's 2012 review findings on strategy effectiveness:
- Printed educational material: 4.3% effectiveness
- Educational meetings: 6%
- Local opinion leaders: 12% (most effective)
- Audit and feedback: 5%

Table provides acupressure-specific examples for each strategy.

### Training
Labeled under CFIR's "Access to knowledge and information." Describes three training packages differentiated by professional roles, plus on-the-job training protocols.

### Engaging Management
Addresses "Leadership Engagement under CFIR," defined as "commitment, involvement, and accountability of leaders and managers." Discusses support mechanisms across executive, middle, and frontline levels.

### Assess
Related to "Reflecting and evaluating under CFIR." Emphasizes defining outcome measures, tracking systems, and regular staff feedback through audits.

### Execute the Implementation
Recommends adoption strategies: practice runs, pilot implementations, and step-approach implementation limiting scope initially.

### External Resources Referenced
- CFIR Guide website for constructs and matching tools
- Joanna Briggs Institute's "PACES" online audit tool

## PHASE 4: SUSTAINMENT

**Introduction:** Implementations often fail to persist after teams depart. Sustainability depends on normalizing practices into routine operations and meeting organizational needs through internal and external factors.

### Outer Factors

**Leadership**
- Concerns external leadership input, such as government support

**Link to other organisations**
- References pressure from external entities (e.g., other hospitals implementing similar practices)

**Funding**
- Addresses implementation costs and available resources
- Considers whether acupuncture/acupressure is cost-neutral
- Examines potential reductions in anti-emetic use and recovery time

### Inner Factors

**Leadership**
- Requires organizational support from leadership
- Recommends engaging leadership from initial phases
- Suggests regular audit reports to leadership
- Advises aligning outcomes with organizational missions

**Fidelity**
- Questions whether practices maintain intended standards
- Example: SeaBand displacement below proper PC6 location
- Recommends onsite monitoring and staff feedback
- Proposes visible, accessible education materials
- Suggests regular education sessions matching routine practice protocols

**Staffing/Support Coaching**
- Emphasizes continuous staff support through education and resources
- Recommends identifying enthusiastic champions per work unit
- Proposes manager reminders to staff
- Advocates visible, accessible education materials
